STA4001 LN8: Stationary Distribution, Recurrence Property

We have reached the core of MC theory: stationary distribution and recurrence property.

Gambler’s Ruin Problem

Consider a DTMC with state space $S={0,1,2,3,4}$ and transition probabilities $P_{00}=P_{44}=1$, $P_{i,i+1}=0.2$, and $P_{i,i-1}=0.8$ for $i\in{1,2,3}$. We call states $0$ and $4$ absorbing states.

Interpretation: A gambler enters a casino with $1$ dollars. He wins $1$ dollar with probability $0.2$, and loses $1$ dollar with probability $0.8$. He plays repeatedly and stops playing when his balance reaches either $0$ or $4$ dollars.

First-Step Method

Probability of Ruin

Let $P_i$ be the probability that the DTMC is absorbed into state $0$ when starting from state $i$.

Take $P_3$ as an example. We go to state $4$ with probability $0.2$ and state $2$ with probability $0.8$. Then
$$
P_3=0.2P_4+0.8P_2
$$
Now $P_4=0$. Hence
$$
P_3=0.8P_2
$$
We calculate $P_2$:
$$
P_2=0.2P_3+0.8P_1
$$
Finally,
$$
P_1=0.2P_2+0.8P_0=0.2P_2+0.8
$$
This gives us a linear system
$$
\begin{pmatrix}
1&-0.2& 0 \ -0.8 & 1 & -0.2 \ 0 & -0.8 & 1
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}
P_1\P_2\P_3
\end{pmatrix}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
0.8\0\0
\end{pmatrix},.
$$
Note that $P_3\approx0.75$ is very high, since the winning probability is only $0.2$.

Suppose now the gambler plays a fair game and stops when his balance reaches $N$. Then $P_1=\frac{N-1}{N}$. To derive this probability, we use our old theorem
$$
\mathbb{E}X_T=X_0=1=0\cdot P_1+N\cdot(1-P_1)
$$
which gives us the desired result.

Expected Ending Time

Let $N_i$ be the expected number of steps to end the game starting from state $i$.

Then
$$
N_3=1+0.2N_4+0.8N_2=1+0.8N_2
$$

$$
N_2=1+0.2N_3+0.8N_1
$$

$$
N_1=1+0.2N_2+0.8N_0=1+0.2N_2
$$

This gives us a linear system that solves $N_1,N_2,N_3$.

Remark. With reasonable assumptions, the matrix is always full-rank.

Stationary Distribution

Previously we talked about transient behaviors; we studied finite-time behavior. Now we study what happens when $t\to\infty$.

Definition. $\pi={\pi_i:i\in S}$ is called a stationary distribution with state space $S$ ($S$ may be infinite) and transition matrix $P$ if

  • $\sum_{i\in S}\pi_i=1$
  • $\pi=\pi P$ (balance equation, $\pi$ viewed as a probability row vector)
  • $\pi\ge 0$

Example. For $P=\begin{pmatrix}0.2&0.8\0.5&0.5\end{pmatrix}$, we have $\pi=(5/13,8/13)$.

Interpretation of balance equation. Suppose the initial state follows a distribution, say, $X_0\sim\pi$ with $P(X_0=i)=\pi_i$. Then
$$
P(X_1=j)=\sum_{i}P(X_0=i)P(X_1=j\mid X_0=i)=\sum_{i}P_{i,j}\pi_i=(\pi P)_j,.
$$
Hence $X_1\sim\pi P$. More generally, $X_n\sim\pi P^n$.

Therefore if $X_0\sim \pi$ with $\pi$ being stationary, then $X_n\sim \pi$ for all $n$. This is why $\pi$ is call stationary.

Examples.

  • Simple symmetric random walk on a circle with $4$ nodes.
    $$
    P=
    \begin{pmatrix}
    0&1/2&0&1/2 \
    1/2&0&1/2&0 \
    0&1/2&0&1/2 \
    1/2&0&1/2&0
    \end{pmatrix},.
    $$
    By symmetry, $\pi=\frac{1}{4}(1,1,1,1)$.

  • Simple symmetric random walk on a circle with $4$ nodes.
    $$
    P=\begin{pmatrix}
    0&1/3&1/3 \
    1/3&0&1/3 \
    1/3&1/3&0
    \end{pmatrix},.
    $$

By symmetry, $\pi=\frac{1}{3}(1,1,1)$.

Connection to Long-term average profit

It seems that $\frac{1}{n}\sum_ig(X_i)$ converges to $\sum_i\pi_ig(i)$ as $n\to\infty$. Note that $X_i$ and $X_{i+1}$ are dependent on each other!

Irreducible DTMC

Definitions.

  • State $i$ reaches state $j$ if there exists a path $i=i_0,i_1,…,i_{n-1},i_n=j$ such that $P_{i_k,i_{k+1}}>0$ for all $k$.
  • A DTMC is called irreducible if every pair of states reach each other.
  • $i$ and $j$ communicates if they reach each other.

Remark. Geometrically, an irreducible DTMC is a connected graph.

Remark. $i$ reaches $j$ $\not\Rightarrow$ $j$ reaches $i$.

Lemma.

  • State $i$ reaches $j$ if there exists an integer $n\ge 1$ if $(P^n)_{ij}>0$.
  • States $i\to j$ and $j\to k$ imply that $i\to k$.

Theorem. An irreducible DTMC has at most one stationary distribution.

Proof. We only prove the case $|S|<\infty$. Suppose $\pi$ and $\pi’$ are stationary distributions. Define
$$
m=\max_i\frac{\pi(i)}{\pi’(i)}=\frac{\pi(i^*)}{\pi’(i^*)},.
$$
(Note that for all $i$, $\pi_i>0$ and $\pi’i>0$. To see this, suppose for contradiction that $\pi_1=\cdots=\pi_k=0$ and $\pi{k+1},…,\pi_n>0$. Then $\pi=\pi P$ implies $P_{\alpha,\alpha}=O$, where $\alpha={k+1,k+2,…,n}$. Then for all $k_1\ge k+1$ and for all $k_2\le k$, state $k_1$ is not able to reach state $k_2$, contradicting the irreducibility assumption.)

By definition,
$$
\begin{align}
\pi(i^*)
&=
\sum_j\pi(j)P_{ji^*} \
&=
\sum_j\frac{\pi(j)}{\pi’(j)}\pi’(j)P_{ji^*} \
&\le
m\sum_j\pi’(j)P_{ji^*} \
&=
m\pi’(i^*) \
&=
\pi(i^*),.
\end{align}
$$
Thus “$\le$” must be “$=$”. Hence
$$
\sum_j\left(\frac{\pi(j)}{\pi’(j)}-m\right)\pi’(j)P_{ji^*}=0 ,.
$$
Since all terms are nonnegative, then for all $j$,
$$
\left(\frac{\pi(j)}{\pi’(j)}-m\right)\pi’(j)P_{ji^*}=0,.
$$
If $P_{ji^*}>0$, then $\frac{\pi(j)}{\pi’(j)}=m$. Noting that for all $k\in\mathbb{N}$, $\pi=\pi P^k$ and $\pi’=\pi’ P^k$, we have
$$
\left(\frac{\pi(j)}{\pi’(j)}-m\right)\pi’(j)(P^k){ji^*}=0,.
$$
By irreducibility, for all $j$, there exists $k$ such that $(P^k){ij}>0$, which implies $\frac{\pi(j)}{\pi’(j)}=m$. Since $\sum_j\pi(j)=\sum_j\pi’(j)=1$, then $m=1$, hence $\pi=\pi’$.

Recurrence and Transience

Definitions. Let
$$
T_i=\min{n\ge 1\mid X_n=i},,
$$
i.e. $T_i$ is the first time to reach state $i$.

State $i$ of a DTMC is recurrent if
$$
P(T_i<\infty\mid X_0=i)=1,.
$$
Otherwise state $i$ is transient, that is, if $P(T_i<\infty\mid X_0=i)<1$ (instead of $P(T_i=\infty\mid X_0=i)=1$).
