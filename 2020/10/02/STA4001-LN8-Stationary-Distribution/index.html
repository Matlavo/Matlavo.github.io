Gambler’s Ruin Problem

Consider a DTMC with state space $S={0,1,2,3,4}$ and transition probabilities $P{00}=P{44}=1$, $P{i,i+1}=0.2$, and $P{i,i-1}=0.8$ for $i\in{1,2,3}$. We call states $0$ and $4$ absorbing states.

Interpretation: A gambler enters a casino with $1$ dollars. He wins $1$ dollar with probability $0.2$, and loses $1$ dollar with probability $0.8$. He plays repeatedly and stops playing when his balance reaches either $0$ or $4$ dollars.

Stationary Distribution

Previously we talked about transient behaviors; we studied finite-time behavior. Now we study what happens when $t\to\infty$.

Definition. $\pi={\pi_i:i\in S}$ is called a stationary distribution with state space $S$ ($S$ may be infinite) and transition matrix $P$ if

  • $\sum_{i\in S}\pi_i=1$
  • $\pi=\pi P$ (balance equation, $\pi$ viewed as a probability row vector)
  • $\pi\ge 0$

Example. For $P=\begin{pmatrix}0.2&0.8\0.5&0.5\end{pmatrix}$, we have $\pi=(5/13,8/13)$.

Interpretation of balance equation. Suppose the initial state follows a distribution, say, $X_0\sim\pi$ with $P(X_0=i)=\pi_i$. Then

Hence $X_1\sim\pi P$. More generally, $X_n\sim\pi P^n$.

Therefore if $X_0\sim \pi$ with $\pi$ being stationary, then $X_n\sim \pi$ for all $n$. This is why $\pi$ is call stationary.

Examples.

  • Simple symmetric random walk on a circle with $4$ nodes.

    By symmetry, $\pi=\frac{1}{4}(1,1,1,1)$.

  • Simple symmetric random walk on a circle with $4$ nodes.

By symmetry, $\pi=\frac{1}{3}(1,1,1)$.

Irreducible DTMC

Definitions.

  • State $i$ reaches state $j$ if there exists a path $i=i0,i_1,…,i{n-1},in=j$ such that $P{ik,i{k+1}}>0$ for all $k$.
  • A DTMC is called irreducible if every pair of states reach each other.
  • $i$ and $j$ communicates if they reach each other.

Remark. Geometrically, an irreducible DTMC is a connected graph.

Remark. $i$ reaches $j$ $\not\Rightarrow$ $j$ reaches $i$.

Lemma.

  • State $i$ reaches $j$ if there exists an integer $n\ge 1$ if $(P^n)_{ij}>0$.
  • States $i\to j$ and $j\to k$ imply that $i\to k$.
