Symmetric Groups

Definition. Let $X$ be a set. A permutation of $X$ is a bijection $\sigma:X\to X$.

Theorem. The set $\text{Sym}(X)={\sigma\mid\sigma\text{ is a permutation of } X}$ is a group under composition of maps.

Notice that the structure of a group $\text{Sym}(X)$ is concerned only with the number of elements in $X$, not what the elements in $X$ are. If $A$ and $B$ have the same cardinality, then $\text{Sym}(A)\cong\text{Sym}(B)$. Thus we can take ${1,2,…,n}$ to be a prototype for a finite set $X$ of $n$ elements.

Decompositions of Permutations

Theorem. Each $\sigma\in S_n$ is a product of disjoint cycles.

We will prove this theorem later with the tools from the theory equivalence class.

Definition. A transposition is a $2$-cycle.

Theorem. Every cycle can be written as a product of transpositions.

Proof. $(i_1,i_2,…,i_n)=(i_1,i_n)\cdots(i_1,i_3)(i_1,i_2)$. Q.E.D.

Corollary. Every permutation is a product of transpositions.

Intuitively, this corollary just states that any rearrangement of $n$ objects can be achieved by successively interchanging pairs of them.

Note, however, that the transpositions may not be disjoint, and a representation of a permutation in this way is not unique. For example, we cam always insert at the beginning the transposition $(1,2)$ twice, for $(1,2)(1,2)$ is the identity permutation. What is true is that the number of transpositions used to represent a given permutation must either always be even or always be odd.

Theorem. In every factorization of $\sigma$ as a product of transpositions, the number of factors is always even or odd.

Proof. Think of elements in $Sn$ as permutations of the $n$ rows of the $n\times n$ identity matrix $I_n$. The identity matrix has determinant $1$, and interchanging any two rows changes the sign of the determinant. Let $A{\sigma}$ be the matrix obtained by a permutation $\sigma$ of rows of $In$. If $A{\sigma}$ could be obtain from $I_n$ by both an even number and an odd number of transpositions of rows, its determinant would have to be $1$ and $-1$, which is impossible. This yields the desired conclusion. Q.E.D.

Remark. Note that if $\sigma$ is a transposition, then $\det(A_{\sigma})=-1$.

Definition. A permutation of a finite set is even or odd according to whether it can be expressed as a product of an even number of transpositions or an odd number transpositions, respectively.

Alternating Groups

Theorem. The collection of all even permutations of ${1,2,…,n}$ forms a subgroup of order $n!/2$ of the symmetric group $S_n$.

Definition. The subgroup of $S_n$ consisting of the even permutations is the $n$th alternating subgroup $A_n$.
