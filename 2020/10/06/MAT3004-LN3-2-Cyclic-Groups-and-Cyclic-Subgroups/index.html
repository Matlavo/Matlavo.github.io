Proof. Obvious.

Definition. The subgroup $\langle g\rangle$ is called the cyclic subgroup of $G$ generated by $g$.

Definition. $G$ is said to be cyclic if $G=\langle g\rangle$ for some $g\in G$. We say that $g$ generates $G$ or is a generator of $G$.

Example. $(\mathbb{Z},+)$ is cyclic. Both $1$ and $-1$ are generators, and they are the only generators.

Example. For every $n\in\mathbb{Z}$, $(n\mathbb{Z},+)=\langle n\rangle$ is a cyclic subgroup of $(\mathbb{Z},+)$.

Properties of Cyclic Groups

Theorem. Every cyclic group is abelian.

Proof. Obvious.

Theorem. $|\langle g\rangle|=|g|$.

Proof 1. Consider the set ${g^n\mid n\in\mathbb{N}}={e,g,g^2,…}$.

  • If $|g|=\infty$, then we will show that $\not\exists\,h\neq k\in\mathbb{N}$ such that $g^h=g^k$. Otherwise $g^{h-k}=e$ (WLOG $h>k$), which contradicts the fact that $|g|=\infty$. Therefore $|\langle g\rangle|=|g|=\infty$.
  • If $|g|=m<\infty$, then by the same argument, $\not\exists 0<h<k<m$ such that $g^h=g^k$. Then ${e,g,g^2,…,g^{m-1}}$ contains $m$ elements. Now for all $n\in\mathbb{Z}$, $n=mq+r$ and thus $g^n=g^r$ with $r=0,1,…,m-1$. Thus $\langle g\rangle={e,g,g^2,…,g^{m-1}}$. Therefore $|\langle g\rangle|=|g|=m$. Q.E.D.

Proof 2. Consider the set ${g^n\mid n\in\mathbb{N}}={e,g,g^2,…}$.

  • If the set has no repeated elements, then $|\langle g\rangle|=|g|=\infty$.
  • If the set has repeated elements, let $m$ be the smallest nonnegative integer such that $g^m=g^k$ for some $0\le k<m$. Then $g^{m-k}=e$. By the minimality of $m$, $m-k=m$. Thus $k=0$ and $g^m=e$. Therefore ${e,g,…,g^{m-1}}$ contains $m$ elements, $|g|=m$, and $|\langle g\rangle|\ge m$. Now for all $n\in\mathbb{Z}$, $n=mq+r$ and thus $g^n=g^r$ with $r=0,1,…,m-1$. Thus $\langle g\rangle={e,g,g^2,…,g^{m-1}}$. Therefore $|\langle g\rangle|=|g|=m$. Q.E.D.

Proposition. If $G=\langle g\rangle$ is of order $n$, then $G={e,g,…,g^{n-1}}$.

Proof. We have $|g|=n$. Thus $G={e,g,…,g^{n-1}}$. Q.E.D.

Theorem. Every subgroup of a cyclic group $G$ is cyclic.

Proof. Let $G=\langle g\rangle$. Suppose $H\le G$.

  • If $H={e}$, then $H=\langle e\rangle$ is cyclic.

  • If $H$ is nontrivial, then let $m$ be the smallest positive integer such that $g^m\in H$. We will show that $H=\langle g^m\rangle$.

    Clearly $\langle g^m\rangle\le H$. For every $g^n\in H$, by Division Theorem, $n=mq+r$ with $0\le r<m$, and $g^n=(g^m)^qg^r$. Then

    By the minimality of $m$, it must hold that $r=0$. Thus $g^n=(g^m)^q\in\langle g^m\rangle$. Hence $H=\langle g^m\rangle$ is cyclic. Q.E.D.

Corollary. The subgroups of $(\mathbb{Z},+)$ are precisely the groups $(n\mathbb{Z},+)$ for all $n\in\mathbb{Z}$.

Proof. $\mathbb{Z}$ is cyclic and $H$ is a subgroup of $\mathbb{Z}$ $\Rightarrow$ $H$ is cyclic $\Rightarrow$ $H=\langle n\rangle$ for some $n\in\mathbb{Z}$ $\Rightarrow$ $H=n\mathbb{Z}$.

Conversely, every $(n\mathbb{Z},+)$ is cyclic. Q.E.D.

Structure of Cyclic Groups

The following theorem describes all cyclic groups up to isomorphism.

Theorem. Let $G$ be a cyclic group. If $|G|=\infty$, then $G\cong(\mathbb{Z},+)$. If $|G|=n$, then $G\cong(\mathbb{Z}_n,+_n)$.

Corollary. All generators of a cyclic group $G=\langle g\rangle$ of order $n$ are precisely $g^r$ with $\gcd(r,n)=1$.

Proof. By the preceding corollary, $\langle g^r\rangle=G=\langle g\rangle$ if and only if $\gcd(r,n)=\gcd(1,n)=1$. Q.E.D.

Miscellaneous

Proposition. Let $a,b\in\mathbb{Z}$ be such that $a^2+b^2\neq 0$. Let $k\in\mathbb{Z}\backslash{0}$. Then $\gcd(ka,kb)=k\gcd(a,b)$.

Proof. By Bezout’s identity, $sa+tb=\gcd(a,b)$, so $s(ka)+t(kb)=k\gcd(a,b)$. Thus $\gcd(ka,kb)\mid k\gcd(a,b)$.

Since $\gcd(a,b)\mid a,b$, then $k\gcd(a,b)\mid ka,kb$, thus $k\gcd(a,b)\mid \gcd(ka,kb)$. We conclude that $\gcd(ka,kb)= k\gcd(a,b)$. Q.E.D.

Proof. WLOG suppose $a,b>0$. Let $d=\gcd(a,b)$ and $k=\text{lcm}(a,b)$. Then $ab/d=(a/d)b=a(b/d)$ is divisible by $a$ and $b$, so $ab/d\ge k$. Thus $ab\ge dk$.
