Group Homomorphism

Definition. Let GGG and G′G'G′ be groups. A group homomorphism is a map ϕ:G→G′\phi:G\to G'ϕ:G→G′ such that

ϕ(ab)=ϕ(a)ϕ(b)∀a,b∈G\phi(ab)=\phi(a)\phi(b)\quad\forall a,b\in G ϕ(ab)=ϕ(a)ϕ(b)∀a,b∈G

If ϕ\phiϕ is bijective, then we say ϕ\phiϕ is an isomorphism from GGG to G′G'G′. We say GGG is isomorphic to G′G'G′, and write G≃GG\simeq GG≃G.

Remark. If ϕ:G→G′\phi:G\to G'ϕ:G→G′ is an isomorphism, then ϕ−1:G′→G\phi^{-1}:G'\to Gϕ−1:G′→G is an isomorphism. Thus G≃G′G\simeq G'G≃G′ if and only if G′≃GG'\simeq GG′≃G.

Proof. For all ϕ(a),ϕ(b)∈G′\phi(a),\phi(b)\in G'ϕ(a),ϕ(b)∈G′,

ϕ−1(ϕ(a)ϕ(b))=ϕ−1(ϕ(ab))=ab=ϕ−1(ϕ(a))ϕ−1(ϕ(b))\phi^{-1}(\phi(a)\phi(b))=\phi^{-1}(\phi(ab))=ab=\phi^{-1}(\phi(a))\phi^{-1}(\phi(b)) ϕ−1(ϕ(a)ϕ(b))=ϕ−1(ϕ(ab))=ab=ϕ−1(ϕ(a))ϕ−1(ϕ(b))

Thus ϕ−1\phi^{-1}ϕ−1 is a homomorphism and thus an isomorphism. Q.E.D.

Remark. Isomorphic objects have the same algebraic structure. They only differ by a relabeling of their elements.

Properties of Isomorphism

Theorem. If G≃HG\simeq HG≃H and GGG is cyclic, then HHH is cyclic.

Proof. Suppose G=⟨g⟩G=\langle g\rangleG=⟨g⟩. Since ϕ\phiϕ is surjective, then for all h∈Hh\in Hh∈H, there exists n∈Zn\in\mathbb{Z}n∈Z such that ϕ(gn)=h\phi(g^n)=hϕ(gn)=h. Then h=ϕ(g)n∈⟨ϕ(g)⟩h=\phi(g)^n\in\langle\phi(g)\rangleh=ϕ(g)n∈⟨ϕ(g)⟩. Thus H=⟨ϕ(g)⟩H=\langle\phi(g)\rangleH=⟨ϕ(g)⟩. Q.E.D.

Theorem. If G≃HG\simeq HG≃H and GGG is abelian, then HHH is abelian.

Proof. For all ϕ(x),ϕ(y)∈H\phi(x),\phi(y)\in Hϕ(x),ϕ(y)∈H, ϕ(x)ϕ(y)=ϕ(xy)=ϕ(yx)=ϕ(y)ϕ(x)\phi(x)\phi(y)=\phi(xy)=\phi(yx)=\phi(y)\phi(x)ϕ(x)ϕ(y)=ϕ(xy)=ϕ(yx)=ϕ(y)ϕ(x). Q.E.D.
